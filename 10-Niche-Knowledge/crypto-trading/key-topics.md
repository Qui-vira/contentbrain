# Crypto Trading — Key Topics Reference

> Each topic summarized in 2-3 sentences. Use as a content idea bank and knowledge primer.

---

## Technical Analysis (TA)

### Support & Resistance
Horizontal price levels where buying (support) or selling (resistance) pressure has historically concentrated. These levels become self-fulfilling because thousands of traders watch the same zones. Flipped support becomes resistance and vice versa — teaching this concept alone is a high-value post.

### Indicators (RSI, MACD, Moving Averages, Bollinger Bands)
Lagging mathematical overlays on price that help confirm trends, momentum, and overbought/oversold conditions. Most retail traders over-rely on indicators without understanding price action underneath. Content that shows indicators as confirmation tools (not crystal balls) builds credibility.

### Chart Patterns (Head & Shoulders, Triangles, Flags, Wedges)
Recurring geometric formations in price that signal continuation or reversal with statistical tendencies. In crypto, patterns break more often due to low liquidity and manipulation — teaching this nuance separates real educators from copycats. Visual breakdowns of patterns on real charts perform extremely well on short-form video.

### Candlestick Patterns
Individual or multi-candle formations (engulfing, doji, hammer, morning star) that signal short-term sentiment shifts. Useful as entry/exit timing tools when combined with higher-timeframe structure. Quick visual explainers of these patterns are reliable engagement content.

### Market Structure (Higher Highs, Lower Lows, Break of Structure)
The backbone of price action analysis — identifying whether price is trending up, down, or ranging by tracking swing points. A "break of structure" (BOS) or "change of character" (ChoCH) signals a potential trend shift. This is the foundation Smart Money Concept (SMC) traders build on.

### Volume Profile
Shows how much trading volume occurred at each price level over a given period, revealing where the real interest sits. High-volume nodes act as magnets; low-volume nodes act as air pockets price moves through quickly. Underrated tool that most retail content ignores — opportunity for differentiation.

## On-Chain Signals

### Whale Tracking
Monitoring large wallet movements via tools like Arkham Intelligence, Nansen, Lookonchain, and Etherscan to front-run or follow institutional-scale positioning. When a known smart-money wallet accumulates an altcoin, it's signal. Content format: "This wallet made $X doing Y — here's what they just bought."

### Exchange Flows (Inflows/Outflows)
Tracking tokens moving onto exchanges (potential sell pressure) or off exchanges (accumulation/self-custody). Large exchange inflows of BTC or ETH historically precede sell-offs; sustained outflows are bullish. CryptoQuant and Glassnode are the go-to data sources.

### Funding Rates
The periodic payment between long and short perp holders that keeps contract prices aligned with spot. Extremely positive funding means the market is overleveraged long (contrarian short signal); deeply negative funding means shorts are crowded. One of the most reliable on-chain trading signals available.

### Open Interest (OI) Analysis
Total notional value of open perpetual futures contracts — tells you how much leverage is in the system. OI rising with price = new money entering (strong trend). OI dropping with price = longs getting liquidated (cascade risk). Pair OI data with funding rates for a complete leverage picture.

### Stablecoin Supply & Flows
Growth in USDT/USDC supply on-chain is a proxy for new capital entering crypto. Stablecoins sitting on exchanges = dry powder ready to buy. Monitoring stablecoin mints and exchange deposits is a macro bullish/bearish signal.

## Liquidation Mechanics

### Liquidation Maps
Visual tools (Coinglass, Hyblock Capital) showing where clusters of leveraged positions will get force-closed at specific prices. Price is magnetically attracted to liquidation clusters because they represent guaranteed order flow. Content showing liquidation heatmaps before and after a price move is extremely engaging — "they came for the liquidity."

### Liquidation Cascades
When a price move triggers liquidations, those forced closures push price further, triggering more liquidations in a chain reaction. This is how crypto produces those violent 10-20% candles in minutes. Explaining this mechanic to an audience builds trust — you're showing them the game behind the game.

## DeFi Trading

### DEX Aggregators
Platforms like Jupiter (Solana) and 1inch (EVM) that route trades across multiple DEXs to find the best execution price. They solve the fragmented liquidity problem and reduce slippage. Content angle: show the price difference between going direct to a DEX vs. using an aggregator.

### MEV (Maximal Extractable Value)
The profit that block builders and searchers extract by reordering, inserting, or censoring transactions within a block. Sandwich attacks, front-running, and back-running are the most common forms — they cost retail traders billions annually. Explaining MEV and how to protect against it (private RPCs, MEV-protected swaps) is high-value educational content.

### Slippage & Price Impact
The difference between expected execution price and actual fill price, caused by insufficient liquidity depth. On small-cap tokens or thin DEX pools, even a $5K swap can move price 3-5%. Teaching audience to check price impact before swapping is a simple, trust-building content piece.

### LP (Liquidity Provider) Strategy
Providing liquidity to AMM pools to earn trading fees and incentives. The core trade-off is impermanent loss — your position underperforms simply holding when prices diverge. Concentrated liquidity (Uniswap V3 style) amplifies both fees earned and IL risk.

## Risk Management

### Position Sizing
Determining how much capital to allocate per trade based on account size, stop distance, and risk tolerance (typically 1-3% of portfolio per trade). This is the single most important factor separating profitable traders from blown accounts. Content that drills this concept repeatedly builds the right audience — serious traders.

### Stop Losses & Invalidation
Pre-defined exit points where your trade thesis is wrong. In crypto, wide stops are often necessary due to volatility and liquidity grabs — tight stops get hunted. Teaching the difference between a stop loss and an invalidation level is an underserved content niche.

### Risk/Reward Ratios
Comparing potential profit to potential loss before entering. A 3:1 R/R means you can be wrong 2 out of 3 times and still profit. Simple concept, but framing actual trades through this lens in content makes your analysis actionable rather than theoretical.

### Correlation Risk
Holding multiple positions that move together (e.g., 5 different altcoins) concentrates risk rather than diversifying it. In crypto, most assets are highly correlated to BTC on downside moves. Teaching audience to stress-test their portfolio for "what happens if BTC drops 30%" is practical risk content.

## Portfolio Construction

### Core-Satellite Model
Hold a core position in high-conviction assets (BTC, ETH, SOL) and allocate smaller satellite positions to higher-risk/higher-reward plays. This balances participation in upside with survivability. Relatable framework for content — audience can immediately apply it.

### Rebalancing & Rotation
Systematically taking profits from outperformers and reallocating to underperformers or stablecoins to lock in gains. In crypto bull markets, the impulse is to let everything ride — content showing the math of rebalancing vs. holding teaches discipline. Sector rotation (e.g., from memecoins to DeFi to AI tokens) is a narrative-driven crypto-specific version.

### Stablecoin Allocation
Keeping a portion of portfolio in stablecoins (USDT, USDC, DAI) as dry powder for dips and as a hedge against drawdowns. The psychological discipline of "sitting in stables" during euphoria is one of the hardest skills in crypto. Content normalizing cash positions as a strategy, not cowardice, resonates deeply.
