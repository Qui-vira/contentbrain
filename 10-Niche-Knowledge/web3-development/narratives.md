# Web3 Development — Active Narratives (Early 2025)

> Narratives drive attention, attention drives engagement. Know these to write timely, relevant content.

---

## Restaking

**What it is:** Validators re-use their staked ETH to secure additional protocols/services (called AVSs — Actively Validated Services). Instead of each new protocol bootstrapping its own validator set, they rent Ethereum's security.

**Key players:**
- **EigenLayer** — pioneered the category, largest by TVL. Live on mainnet with AVSs launching.
- **Symbiotic** — backed by Lido/Paradigm, more flexible and permissionless design. Positioned as the "open" alternative.
- **Karak** — multi-asset restaking (not just ETH).

**Why it matters for content:** Restaking is one of the most debated topics in Ethereum. Bulls say it extends ETH's security moat. Bears say it creates systemic risk (cascading slashing). Either angle is high-engagement content. The "points meta" around restaking drove massive TVL inflows in 2024 and generated strong opinions.

## Modular Blockchains

**What it is:** Unbundling the four functions of a blockchain (execution, settlement, consensus, data availability) into specialized layers. Opposite of "monolithic" chains that do everything on one layer.

**Key players:**
- **Celestia** — the OG modular DA layer. Chains post data to Celestia instead of Ethereum for cheaper DA costs.
- **Avail** — Polygon-originated DA layer, competing directly with Celestia.
- **EigenDA** — DA layer built on EigenLayer's restaking infrastructure.

**Why it matters for content:** The modular vs. monolithic debate is one of crypto's great divides. Ethereum is going modular (L2-centric roadmap). Solana is the monolithic counter-narrative. This framing generates strong tribal engagement — perfect for hot takes and thread content.

## Chain Abstraction

**What it is:** Hiding the multi-chain complexity from users. Instead of manually bridging, switching networks, and managing gas on five different chains — users just do the thing, and the infra handles routing under the hood.

**Key players:**
- **Particle Network** — universal accounts across chains, chain-level account abstraction.
- **Socket** — modular interoperability protocol, powers chain abstraction for apps.
- **NEAR** — chain signatures let NEAR accounts sign transactions on any chain.
- **Connext/Everclear** — cross-chain liquidity settlement.

**Why it matters for content:** Chain abstraction is the UX thesis — crypto won't go mainstream if users need to understand L1s, L2s, bridges, and gas tokens. Posts that explain "the internet doesn't make you pick which server to use" land well. Developer content here focuses on building apps that work across chains without users knowing.

## AI Agents On-Chain

**What it is:** Autonomous AI agents that can hold wallets, execute transactions, trade, govern DAOs, and interact with smart contracts without human intervention.

**Key players:**
- **Virtuals Protocol** — platform for creating and tokenizing AI agents. Exploded on Base in late 2024.
- **Autonolas (OLAS)** — framework for building decentralized autonomous agent services.
- **ai16z / ElizaOS** — open-source AI agent framework with on-chain capabilities.
- **Spectral, Bittensor** — AI infrastructure plays adjacent to the agent narrative.

**Why it matters for content:** AI x Crypto is the most attention-rich intersection in the space right now. The meme coin angle (agent-launched tokens) gets clicks, but the real content opportunity is explaining what agents can actually DO: manage treasuries, rebalance portfolios, execute complex DeFi strategies 24/7. Skeptic angle also works: "Most 'AI agents' are just bots with a wrapper."

## Appchains

**What it is:** Application-specific blockchains — each major app gets its own chain instead of sharing blockspace on a general-purpose L2. Frameworks like Arbitrum Orbit, OP Stack, and Polygon CDK make spinning up a custom chain trivially easy.

**Key players:**
- **Arbitrum Orbit** — Arbitrum-based appchains (Xai for gaming, Sanko, etc.)
- **OP Stack** — powers Base, Zora, Mode, Worldcoin chain, and dozens more.
- **Polygon CDK** — ZK-powered custom chains (Immutable zkEVM, Astar zkEVM).

**Why it matters for content:** The "every app becomes a chain" narrative challenges the idea that we need general-purpose L2s at all. Good debate content. Also raises the fragmentation problem — 500 chains means 500 disconnected liquidity pools (ties back to chain abstraction narrative).

## The "Fat App" Thesis

**What it is:** A counter-narrative to the classic "fat protocol" thesis (which said value accrues to base layers like ETH, not apps). The fat app thesis argues that killer applications will capture more value than the chains they run on — especially as infrastructure becomes commoditized and cheap.

**Key argument:** When blockspace is abundant and near-free (thanks to L2s and modular DA), the scarce resource shifts from infrastructure to distribution and user relationships. Apps with users win.

**Examples cited:** Uniswap, Aave, and Maker generate more revenue than most L2s. Friend.tech briefly generated more fees than Ethereum L1.

**Why it matters for content:** This narrative flips the standard crypto investment framework on its head. Great for contrarian takes and "here's what most people get wrong" style posts. Developer angle: if you're a builder, build apps not infra — the infra war is already won.

## Intent-Based Architectures

**What it is:** Instead of users manually constructing transactions (pick a DEX, set slippage, choose a route), users declare an intent ("swap 1 ETH for max USDC") and a network of solvers competes to execute it optimally.

**Key players:**
- **UniswapX** — intent-based trading for Uniswap, solvers fill orders.
- **CowSwap** — batch auctions + solver competition, MEV protection.
- **Across** — intent-based cross-chain bridging.
- **Essential** — intent-based blockchain architecture from the ground up.

**Why it matters for content:** Intents solve the "crypto is too complicated" problem at the transaction level. Also directly attacks the MEV problem (solvers compete for users, not against them). Content framing: "Intents are DoorDash for transactions — you say what you want, someone else figures out how."

## Narrative Content Strategy

When writing about narratives, the highest-performing formats are:

1. **Explainer threads** — "What is [narrative] and why should you care?"
2. **Comparison posts** — "Restaking vs. traditional staking" or "Modular vs. monolithic"
3. **Contrarian takes** — "Why [hot narrative] might be overhyped"
4. **Builder spotlights** — Profile a project within the narrative, explain what they're doing differently
5. **Timeline posts** — "The restaking narrative in 5 key moments"

All of these narratives interconnect. The best content draws lines between them (e.g., "Chain abstraction only works if appchains succeed" or "AI agents need account abstraction to function").
